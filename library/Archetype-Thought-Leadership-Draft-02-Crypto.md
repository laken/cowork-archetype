# Digital Asset Firms Are the Highest-Value Targets on the Internet. Most Act Like They Aren't.

*By Andy Laken, Fractional CTO at Archetype Consulting*

---

You're running a digital asset firm. Real capital, real systems — fund NAV, investor wires, wallet infrastructure, trading accounts. A small, trusted team. And almost certainly a security posture assembled for speed, not for the adversary you're actually facing.

That gap is what attackers are counting on.

## The asymmetry

Digital asset firms sit at the worst possible intersection: maximum target value, minimum defensive infrastructure.

You're holding or moving significant capital across wallets, exchanges, and custodians, with thinner operational layers than a traditional financial institution. The adversaries on the other side aren't opportunists. State-sponsored groups, sophisticated criminal organizations, and highly coordinated social engineering operations have identified digital asset firms as among the most efficient targets on the internet. Real money, real access, lean teams, no IT department.

In 2025, state-sponsored attackers alone stole over $2 billion from the digital asset space. That number doesn't include the attempts that almost worked, or the incidents that got quietly resolved.

## What the attacks look like now

The threat has moved well past phishing emails with suspicious links. Working directly with digital asset funds and talking with peers across the industry, here's what I'm seeing.

Telegram and WhatsApp account compromise, used to impersonate team members or trusted contacts. The attacker has mapped your org from LinkedIn. The messages are contextually perfect.

Deepfake video calls where a "counterparty" or "investor" requests sensitive information or an urgent wire. The face is generated in real time from public photos. The voice is cloned from podcast appearances.

Hyper-personalized phishing built from scraped social data, public filings, and blockchain analytics — then cleaned up by AI to remove the tells that used to make these obvious.

Business email compromise where the attacker sits in your communications for weeks, learns the patterns, and strikes when a wire or key approval is in motion.

## Why the standard response isn't enough

Most digital asset firms I talk to have done something. MFA on the important accounts. Maybe a password manager. A hardware wallet policy followed inconsistently.

That's a collection of individual decisions made when something felt urgent. It's not a program, and it has no owner.

The gaps that get exploited aren't exotic. A former employee still holding exchange API access. A contractor with admin rights on systems they no longer touch. Personal devices connected to trading infrastructure with no visibility. A Telegram account without two-factor authentication — in an industry where Telegram is the primary attack surface.

Sophisticated adversaries don't need a sophisticated entry point. They need one gap. In a firm that's been moving fast for three years, there are usually several.

## What closing the gap actually takes

Here's the part that surprises most people: this is not a large undertaking.

You don't need an enterprise security team. You need a program right-sized for a lean digital asset operation — a small number of high-impact decisions, implemented properly, maintained by someone who actually owns them.

Start with access. Who has credentials to what — exchanges, custodians, cloud infrastructure — and does that list reflect reality today? In most firms I've worked with, nobody can fully answer that question. That's the first problem.

Add endpoint visibility: detection and response software on every device that touches your infrastructure. A few dollars per device per month. Without it, you're blind to what's actually happening on your team's machines.

Lock down your communication channels. Two-factor authentication on every Telegram and WhatsApp account, hardened against SIM-swap. Clear internal protocols for verifying identity before any sensitive action.

Then make someone accountable for all of it — not as a side project, not shared between your head of trading and your most technical analyst. One person, reviewing and evolving the program as threats change. For most firms at this stage, that's ten to twenty hours a month from a senior technologist. Not a full-time hire.

The firms I've worked with have closed these gaps in weeks, not quarters. The adversary is sophisticated. The defense doesn't have to be complicated — it just has to exist, be implemented correctly, and have an owner.
