# Why Your Growth-Stage Company Is a Cybersecurity Target (And What to Do About It)

*By Andy Laken, Fractional CTO at Archetype Consulting*

---

You built your company with a lean team, a bias toward speed, and a healthy disrespect for bureaucracy. You spun up Google Workspace, a CRM, a handful of SaaS tools, and got to work. Nobody hired an IT person because nobody needed one. That approach got you here, and it was the right call.

But the threat landscape has changed faster than most growth-stage companies realize, and the playbook that worked at ten people starts breaking at thirty.

## The uncomfortable math

Cybercriminals have figured out that growth-stage companies are the most valuable targets per unit of effort. Here's why.

You have real money moving through real systems — payroll, vendor payments, customer transactions, investor wires. You have valuable data: customer records, financial models, intellectual property, strategic plans. And you have almost no security infrastructure protecting any of it.

Large enterprises have dedicated security teams, endpoint detection, network segmentation, and compliance programs. Early-stage startups don't have enough value to be worth targeting. Growth-stage companies sit in the gap: high-value targets with startup-grade defenses.

Adversaries know this. In the digital asset space alone, state-sponsored hackers stole over $2 billion in 2025. But it's not just crypto — sophisticated attacks are hitting healthcare startups, fintech companies, and any business that handles sensitive data or moves significant capital.

## What the attacks actually look like now

The threat has evolved beyond the Nigerian prince email. What I'm seeing in practice — across my own clients and colleagues in the industry — looks more like this.

An attacker compromises a team member's Telegram or WhatsApp account and uses it to impersonate them to others in the company. They've studied your org chart from LinkedIn. They know who reports to whom. The messages are perfectly written and contextually appropriate.

Deepfake video calls where a "business partner" or "investor" asks for sensitive information or an urgent wire transfer. The face is generated in real-time from publicly available photos and video. The voice is cloned from podcast appearances or conference talks.

Phishing that's hyper-personalized using data scraped from social media, public filings, and data broker databases — then polished by AI to eliminate the grammatical errors that used to be the tell.

Business email compromise where the attacker sits quietly in your email for weeks, learning communication patterns, and then strikes at the perfect moment — an invoice that matches a real vendor, a wire request that looks exactly like the last one the CFO approved.

These aren't theoretical. I've seen every one of them in the past twelve months.

## Why "we'll deal with it later" is the wrong call

Most growth-stage leaders I talk to know they should be doing more about security. But it's never urgent enough to prioritize over product, sales, or fundraising — until something happens. The problem is that by the time it's urgent, the damage is done.

There's also a second-order cost that's less visible but increasingly real: your customers and investors are starting to ask. Enterprise customers send security questionnaires before signing contracts. Investors are doing technical due diligence that includes security posture. Regulators in fintech, healthcare, and financial services are tightening requirements.

If you can't answer those questions, you're not just exposed to attackers — you're leaving revenue on the table.

## What a real security program looks like at your scale

Here's the good news: you don't need an enterprise security team. You need a program that's right-sized for a growth-stage company. That means making a small number of high-impact decisions and implementing them properly. In my experience, the foundation comes down to five things.

**Know who has access to what.** This sounds basic, but I have yet to encounter a growth-stage company that can actually answer this question. Former employees still have active accounts. Contractors have admin access they don't need. Personal devices are connected to production systems with no visibility or control. Start here.

**Put an endpoint detection and response (EDR) tool on every device.** This is the single highest-ROI security investment. It costs a few dollars per device per month and gives you visibility into what's actually happening on your team's machines. If something malicious lands, you know immediately.

**Protect your network perimeter.** Tools like Cloudflare Zero Trust let you control who can access what, from where, on what device. You're no longer relying on the assumption that everyone on your network should be trusted.

**Have a plan for when something goes wrong.** Not a 50-page incident response playbook — a simple document that answers: who do we call, what do we shut down, how do we communicate, and how do we recover. Rehearse it once. You'll be better prepared than 90% of companies your size.

**Make security someone's job.** Not your CEO's side project. Not the most technical engineer's spare-time concern. Someone who wakes up thinking about this, reviews access quarterly, responds to alerts, and keeps the program evolving as threats change.

That someone doesn't need to be a full-time hire. For most companies at this stage, a senior technologist working ten to twenty hours a month is enough — provided they actually own it and are accountable for outcomes.

## The bottom line

The gap between what's targeting growth-stage companies and what those companies have in place to defend themselves is wider than it's ever been. The attacks are more sophisticated, more personal, and more financially devastating. The defenses don't have to be exotic or expensive — they just have to exist, be implemented properly, and be maintained by someone who knows what they're doing.

If you're a CEO or COO reading this and feeling that familiar twinge of unease — the one that shows up every time you see a breach in the news and think "that could be us" — that instinct is correct. The good news is that closing this gap is more achievable than you think. The bad news is that every month you wait, the adversaries get better.
