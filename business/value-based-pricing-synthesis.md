# Value-Based Pricing — Synthesis for Archetype Consulting

*Compiled March 2026 from Jonathan Stark, Blair Enns, Ron Baker, Alan Weiss, and Pia Silva.*
*Purpose: Working reference for designing Archetype's engagement model and pricing.*

---

## The Core Shift

Every methodology agrees on one thing: stop anchoring price to your time. Hourly billing punishes expertise — the better you get, the faster you work, the less you earn. The alternative is anchoring price to the value of the outcome for the buyer.

This requires answering a different question before quoting anything. Not "how long will this take me?" but "what is this outcome worth to them?"

---

## Jonathan Stark — The Mechanics

Stark's contribution is making value pricing practical for solo consultants. His core mechanic is a two-number test:

**Number 1 — Your floor:** Estimate hours × your rate. This is the minimum you'd accept. It's not the price; it's the floor.

**Number 2 — Their ceiling:** Estimate the value of the outcome to the client, then divide by 10. The divide-by-10 is a rough heuristic for "10% of value delivered is a fair price." It's not precise science, but it reorients the question correctly.

**Quote the higher of the two.** If Number 2 is significantly larger than Number 1 — say, 5–10×  — you're underpricing if you bill hourly.

### The Discovery Conversation

You can't run the two-number test without surfacing the value number first. Stark's discovery conversation centers on "why" questions: *Why this project? Why now? Why you?* These pull out the business outcome the client is really buying, which they rarely volunteer on their own.

### Fixed Quotes, Not Estimates

Once you price, the quote is a quote — not an estimate. "You won't pay a dime more than this no matter how long it takes me." The fixed price transfers risk from the client to you, which is the point. You're betting on your own expertise.

### Three Options

Stark recommends presenting three tiers of options with increasing scope and value. This moves the conversation from "yes or no" to "which one," and anchors the client's perception of your work against your own higher options (not against competitors).

### Payment Terms

Ideal terms: 100% upfront. If the client pushes back, use it as a negotiating concession. Installment payments are a last resort, not the default.

## Blair Enns — The Value Conversation

Enns (Win Without Pitching) contributes the most rigorous framework for the pre-pricing conversation. He calls it the value conversation, and he argues it's the most valuable skill in all of professional services.

### Structure

1. **Desired Future State (DFS):** Get the client to articulate, specifically, what success looks like. Not "better security" — what does better security enable? What risk does it eliminate? What deal does it unlock? What does the CIO stop losing sleep over?

2. **Metrics of Success:** How will they know the work succeeded? These metrics are the value levers — they're also what you price against.

3. **Price after the conversation, not before.** Enns is explicit: present options only after the value conversation is complete. You can't price intelligently until you understand what the outcome is worth to them.

### The Key Discipline

Don't skip the value conversation to get to the proposal faster. That's the failure mode — quoting before you understand value, then defending a number that wasn't grounded in anything.

## Ron Baker and Alan Weiss — The Intellectual Foundations

Baker (*Implementing Value Pricing*) and Weiss (*Value-Based Fees*) are the original sources Stark and Enns draw from.

**Baker's core argument:** Price is a function of perceived value, not cost. Cost accounting — figuring out your costs and adding a margin — is appropriate for manufacturing. It's the wrong model for expertise. The value of a consultant's insight is not related to how long it took them to develop it.

**Weiss's key move:** Frame engagements around outcomes and relationships, not deliverables and hours. Retainers are superior to projects for both parties — the client gets access and responsiveness; the consultant gets predictable revenue and the ability to deliver value without nickel-and-diming every hour.

**Weiss on fees:** He recommends providing three options in every proposal, each with a different scope and outcome, with the middle option being the one you actually want to sell. Clients self-select, and the anchor effect of the highest option makes the middle feel reasonable.

## Pia Silva — Productized Services

Silva's contribution is different in kind from the above. She's not primarily teaching value pricing — she's teaching how to eliminate the value conversation by pre-defining the offering.

### The Productized Model

A productized service has:
- Fixed scope (defined deliverables, not open-ended engagement)
- Fixed price (no negotiation, no estimation)
- Fixed timeline
- Defined intake and delivery process

The buyer knows exactly what they're getting and what they'll pay before the first real conversation. This removes friction, eliminates scope creep, and lets you scale without proportionally scaling your time.

### Where It Works

Productized pricing works when your buyer population is sufficiently uniform that the value is predictable. If every client is roughly similar in size, situation, and what the outcome is worth to them, you can set one price that works across the board and accept that you'll occasionally leave money on the table.

### Where It Breaks Down

It breaks down when buyers vary significantly — in size, sophistication, AUM, risk profile, or what the outcome enables for them. A $50M fund and a $500M fund are not the same buyer for a security program, even if the deliverable looks similar. Charging them the same price means underpricing one of them, possibly badly.

## The Synthesis — Where Archetype Sits

Archetype is not in a pure productized scenario, and not in a pure bespoke value-pricing scenario. The working model is a **structured engagement with a flexible price**:

- The *scope* is roughly knowable in advance (the Hyla/Amphibian program is a real template)
- The *price* should vary by buyer because the value varies significantly
- The *first conversation* is therefore doing real pricing work — it's a value conversation, not just qualification

This means the first call with an inbound referral has a job: surface the Desired Future State, identify what's at stake (AUM, regulatory exposure, recent threat experience, investor pressure), and understand what resolution of those stakes is worth to the buyer before any number is named.

The entry-point offering can be positioned as productized (clean scope, defined deliverable, fixed timeline) while being priced per-engagement based on what the discovery conversation reveals.

### The Practical Rule

Don't name a price until you've answered: *If this program works perfectly, what does that make possible for this specific buyer that isn't possible today?* The answer to that question is the value. The price is a fraction of it.
