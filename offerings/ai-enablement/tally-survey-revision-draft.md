## Revise

### 5. Morning workflow → Make it sharper

**Current:** "Describe your morning workflow."
**Problem:** Ty gave a thorough answer, but it described *everything he does* — portfolio management, platform building, risk frameworks, allocation models. Useful background, but didn't help you prioritize the session or pick what to demo.

**Revised:**
> Walk me through the first 30 minutes of your workday. What do you open, what do you scan, and what's the first decision you make?

**Why:** Forces a concrete sequence instead of a role description. Ty's answer would have been "TradingView, CoinMarketCap, Delphi, check overnight moves, decide if anything needs immediate attention" — and you'd have known the morning briefing demo needed to map to *that* sequence, not be a standalone report.

### 6. Concerns → Split into two questions

**Current:** "What are your concerns?"
**Problem:** Ty's answer ("file contamination, security leaks, fragmented data") mixed operational anxiety with data architecture problems. Both matter, but for different parts of the session.

**Revised (6a):**
> What's the worst thing that could happen if this goes wrong? (Be specific — a file gets corrupted, credentials leak, something gets sent that shouldn't be, etc.)

**Revised (6b):**
> What's messy about your current data setup that you wish someone would just fix? (Fragmented across drives, inconsistent formats, manual copy-paste between tools, etc.)

**Why:** 6a surfaces the fear you need to address in the security walkthrough. 6b surfaces the pain point you can actually solve with connectors and skills. Different problems, different parts of the session.

### 7. Success definition → Make it falsifiable

**Current:** "What does success look like?"
**Problem:** Ty's answer was aspirational but unfalsifiable: "low friction setup… move quickly and productively… leapfrog missteps." You can't tell after the session whether you delivered that.

**Revised:**
> If this session goes well, what's one specific thing you'll be able to do afterward that you can't do right now?

**Why:** Forces a concrete deliverable. Ty might have said "have a weekly risk report that runs automatically" or "know exactly how my data is protected." Either answer gives you a clear target to hit and a clear way to evaluate whether you did.

## Add New

These are the questions the Ty session proved you need. Each one maps to a specific moment where you were flying blind.

### 8. Delegation comfort (NEW)

> Think about a task you do regularly that takes real time but not much judgment — formatting a report, pulling numbers from three sources into one view, triaging email. Now think about a task where your judgment IS the work — evaluating a manager, deciding on an allocation, reading a market regime.
>
> Where do you draw the line? What would you happily hand off to an AI assistant tomorrow, and what would you never let it touch?

**Why this matters:** Ty's "more noise, not a productivity boost" reaction to the morning briefing came from this boundary. He didn't want a daily AI report he hadn't learned to trust. The session would have been shaped differently if you'd known his delegation comfort was "incremental, trust-building" rather than "automate everything you can."

### 9. Technical depth preference (NEW)

> How do you want to spend our time together? Pick the one that sounds most like you:
>
> - **Show me what's possible.** Walk me through demos and use cases so I understand the landscape before I commit to anything.
> - **Build something with me.** Pick one real task from my actual work and let's get it working in the session.
> - **Set me up and get out of the way.** Configure the tools, show me the basics, and let me explore on my own.

**Why this matters:** "This meeting could be more technical" was Ty's mid-session feedback. He wanted option B. The session was structured as option A. This question lets you calibrate the run-of-show before you walk in.

### 10. Tool adoption style (NEW)

> When you adopt a new tool, which pattern sounds more like you?
>
> - **All in.** I commit, learn everything, and push it until I've found the edges.
> - **One thing at a time.** Show me one win, let me get comfortable, then show me the next.
> - **Skeptical until proven.** I need to see it work on my actual problem before I believe the pitch.

**Why this matters:** Predicts how to sequence the engagement. Ty is clearly "one thing at a time" / "skeptical until proven." The session tried to cover too much breadth. If you'd known this, you'd have gone deep on one deliverable instead of demoing five capabilities.

### 11. The one thing (NEW)

> If you could wave a wand and have one recurring task just *done* — correctly, on time, every week — what would it be?

**Why this matters:** This is the convergence of questions 5, 7, and 8. It identifies the highest-value automation target in their own words. For Ty, this probably would have been the weekly risk limit review or the IC Analytics Pack assembly. Either one gives you a clear Session 1 deliverable target and a concrete "did this work?" evaluation after.
