## Voice and Writing Rules

These apply to all content — outreach, website copy, LinkedIn posts, blog articles, proposals:

### What to aim for (positive model)

**Before writing any article:** Get the argument in one paragraph from Andy — what he wants the reader to believe by the end, and the one fact or moment that made it real for him. Get a benchmark piece (one article in the right register). Confirm the specific audience. Without these, first drafts will be structurally defensible but inert.

**What makes prose hold together:** Every paragraph follows the previous one for a logical reason, not an organizational one. The question to ask at every transition is "why does this come next?" not "what's the next section?" Organizational transitions produce competent outlines. Logical transitions produce articles that accumulate.

**The generating failure mode:** Imposing a structure (story → framework → diagnosis → close) and filling it in section by section. Each part holds up locally; the piece doesn't build. The fix is to derive structure from the argument, not impose it before the argument is clear.

The voice reference is the site copy at archetypeconsulting.io. Key qualities drawn from that source:

- **Conversational mid-length register.** Not clipped minimalism, not long-form journalism. Sentences complete a thought and stop. No decorative clauses tacked on for rhythm.
- **Second person for the prospect's experience, first person for your own work.** "You're growing a business with a smart, lean team." / "I declared a code red and proposed a comprehensive security program." Clean switches. No confusion about whose experience is being described.
- **The specific over the vague, always.** Reach for the actual number, the actual timeline, the actual tool — not the superlative. "exceeded user trust goals by 49%," "pivot in four months," "Software Architect who has now led their dev team for three years."
- **Understatement as a rhetorical move.** "As we saw colleagues in the industry get catastrophically hit" does more work than any amount of "this is real and it's happening." Never announce urgency — describe the situation until the reader feels it.
- **Hide the structure.** The case study copy reads like someone telling you what happened, not a case study formatted as a case study. The problem section doesn't announce itself — it puts the reader in the situation. Blog posts should do the same: the architecture should be invisible.
- **No rhetorical questions. No "here's the thing." No stacked adjectives.** No "I help X do Y so they can Z."

### What to avoid (prohibitions)

1. **Pain-first, not credentials-first.** Write from inside the prospect's experience, not describing it from outside.
2. **Direct language over marketing polish.** If it sounds like a brochure, cut it. If it's true and specific, keep it.
3. **Eliminate, don't fix.** Weak sections get cut, not patched.
4. **No two-part rhetorical constructions** that perform insight instead of delivering it. This entire family of constructions is banned: "That's not X, it's Y." / "This isn't X, it's Y." / "It's not X — it's Y." / "Not X. Y." They announce a reframe instead of delivering one, and are among the most recognizable LLM-generated writing patterns in existence. Kill on sight, in any form.
5. **No "not just" or "more than" setups** that expand beyond expectations.
6. **No "these aren't theoretical" or "this is happening to firms like yours"** constructions — or any variant that performs urgency instead of demonstrating it. Show the threat; don't announce that it's real.
6a. **No "X doesn't need Y, they need Z" reversals.** This includes variants like "Sophisticated attackers don't need a sophisticated entry point, they need any entry point" — a rhetorical construction that announces an insight rather than delivering one. It is a recognizable LLM tic and must never appear in any content.
6b. **No noun-phrase fragments standing in as sentences.** Every sentence must have a subject and a verb. Constructions like "A contractor with admin rights that outlasted the project." or "Personal devices connected to trading infrastructure." are not sentences and must never appear in prose. If examples belong in a list, put them in a bulleted list. In running prose, they must be integrated into real sentences that explain the context, not itemized as fragments.
7. **Understated professionalism** — not hustle culture aesthetics, not clever phrasing, not buzzwords.
8. **Authentic over polished.** Real portrait over AI-generated. Direct copy over clever copy.
9. **n-dashes not m-dashes.** Oxford commas. Mailchimp Content Style Guide as reference for web writing.
10. **Ship at 70%.** Diminishing returns on refinement vs. urgent need for pipeline.
11. The reference standard for copy: [Big Whoop Tax](https://www.dgsiegel.net) (David Siegel) — visceral, authentic, pain-focused. This is a structural/flow reference for website design, not a prose voice model for blog posts.

## Key Insights (Use These to Inform All Content)

- Security breaches and operational failures are the **same worry** — breaches happen through mundane failures (ex-employees retaining access, misconfigured permissions) not sophisticated attacks.
- The "ownership gap" is the core problem: technical responsibilities fall between developers, contractors, and ops leads. Nobody owns the whole picture.
- Growth-stage companies are the **highest-value targets per unit of effort** for attackers: real money, real data, startup-grade defenses.
- AI pressure comes from investors, not just FOMO. Framing: "your investors are asking about your AI strategy."
- Enterprise security questionnaires are a **revenue blocker** — companies that can't answer them lose deals.
- For fractional CTO positioning: one accountable person beats three specialists because nobody gets lost between the cracks.
